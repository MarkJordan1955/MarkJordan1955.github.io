Homework 9 -  Floodplain Features

    Narraguagus River: Route 9 Bridge to Beddington Lake Delta

Part 1 -   Delineate Active Channels, Floodplain Extent and Geomorphic units


    The upper portion of the Narraguagus River from the
Route 9 bridge to the Beddington Lake delta has a large (relative to this part
of the river) floodplain on river-right and several small pocket floodplains on
river-left. The floodplain and the largest flooplain features were likely developed
in a proglacial, fluvial environment as the Laurentide Ice Sheet retreated to the
interior of Maine. The main channel is thopught to be the product of log drive
era river abuse. Prior to restoration efforts, the main channel of the upper
portion of the study area appeared to be over-widened, featureless and heavily
armored (see Figure 6 in Homework 6) with very little local sediment movement
and void of in-stream gemorphic units. The lower part of the study area, despite
the lack of an upstream sediment source, has more geomorphic character, but not
much in the way of traditional floodplains.

    Homework 9 is based on the 2017 1-m DEM produced by Noah
Snyder, Ph.D., of Boston College (provided to me by the US Fish & Wildlife
Services), and augmented by the post-restoration bathymetry survey

Figure 2. Pro-glacial mid channel bar or alluvial fan?

    The upstream end of this reach has been anthropogentically modified by a series of infrastructure projects, including
an old mill and the current bridge. The bridge is located at the end of a somewhat confined section of river, below the bridge
the valley widens allowing for the pro-glacial flows and sediments to settle out. The plan view contours and cross-section A-A
(Figure 2, panels B and C) show the current floodplain is a concave down saddle in the transverse plane that is bisected by the
modern, incised river. I am leaning towards identifying this feature as a midlle channel bar based on the assumption that it
was deposited by pro-glacial flows that filled the valley from bank to bank. Regardless of its exact formation mechanism, the
feature is now the modern floodplain.


Figure 3.a. Diagonal Bars or ice jam flow relief channels?

    The downstream end of the valley segment narrows, the floodplain appears to conist of a set of three diagonal bars.
The current floodplain sediments were likely deposited by pro-glacial flows. The three cross-cutting channels may have
been formed as part of a set of diagonal bars during a pro-glacier flow event or may have been carved by modern flood flows.
See Figure 3.b. for one likely scenario for producing channel forming flows across the floodplain.


Figure 3.b. Floodplain channel at at bankfull flowin late winter.

    The floodplain channel is at bankfull flow because of an ice jam in the main channel. Some of the places ice jams tend
to form are at tight bends or a channel/valley constrictions, both of which occur just downstream of the three cross-cutting
floodplain channels.

Figure 5. Floodplain Slope

    A majority of the surfaces associated with the river-right floodplain and the downstream mid channel bars lie on a
plane that slopes in the downstream direction at 0.0066 ft/ft. This is further evidence that the modern floodplain and
paleo bars were once part of a river bed at a uniform slope.
