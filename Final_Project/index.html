Part 1 - Mapping Geomorphic Units from Imagery in QRiS:


For an experienced QGIS user, the mapping of geomorphic units was straight forward. Five types of geomorphic units were mapped, including:
pools, runs/glides, a riffle, mid-channel bars and bank-attached bars. Using high resolution imagery allows the user to see exposed features
and map them. The challenges are related to water depths and vegetation, only those features, or portions of those features, that are exposed
can be mapped with any accuracy. Identifying features can be somewhat subjective, for example, at higher deth/flow levels what looks like a
run/glide may very much look like a rifle.

  The analysis portion of the exercise, appears interesting, although I must of have slept through the explanation of "Mask Polygons", I need to
re-read/re-listen to get more meaningful numeric results.

  Many of my professional projects are intended to re-activate geomorphic processes that have been lost or severely reduced. Clients and funding
organizations often want a quantative assessment of the changes in the formation of these types of features, between pre aned post construction.
QRiS appears to be a great tool to accomplish this.

Part 2 - :


Our local river, the Presumpscot, is 26 miles long and currently has 9 dams on it (there used to be more). Even if the Presumpscot wasn't
currently covered with ice, the amount of sediment movement and the formation of natural geomorphic units is very limited (although it does
get some fun ice features occasionally, see Figure 2).


Figure 2. Presumpscot River Ice Disk (https://www.thisiscolossal.com/2019/01/gigantic-cicular-ice-patch).

Instead of constructing a field map, I describe several geomorphic units associated with a meander bend that appears to have an over-active tendancy
to form chute cut-off channels or to be in transition to a circular meander pool (Andrle, 1994). I also map the well behaved (other than it moved
laterally 34-feet in two years) downstream meander bend and point bar.   Both these features are mapped using a Digital Elevation Model (DEM)
with a sub 1-foot resolution.   The DEM was developed using drone collected, green LiDAR data for a section of the Konkapot River in New Marlborough, MA.
The project that the DEM was developed for is described in Part 3 (optional reading), the site is also great habitat for the 'threatened' Wood Turtle.
A site location map and a native resident are shown below. I spent 3+ weeks on-site directing the construction of 3 engineered log jams, new wetland swale,
wrapped soil lifts, etc. designed to stabilize the river bank, arrest a head cut and protect/improve habitat.
