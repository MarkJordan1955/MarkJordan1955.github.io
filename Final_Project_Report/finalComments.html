Catchment Scale Geomorphic Relationships

The upper Narraguagus River does not fit the classic model of "erosional-transport-depositional" zones from upstream to downstream. Rather, the headwaters consit of several lakes and a large wetland. Although there is plenty of sediment in the river's the bed and banks, the river typically can not mobilize these sediments because the resisting forces exceed the impelling forces that would otherwise mobilze these sediments.

The typical river banks are described below:

  • Tree lined with coarse sediments - The tree's root system wrapping around coarse sediments provides for a strong embankment.

  • Well vegetated wetlands - The associated root systems produce erosion resistent banks and floodplains.

Much of the river bed between The Oxbow and Beddington Lake is at a very low grade, on the order of 0.002 ft/ft, while the steeper sections of river are often only 0.005 ft/ft. In addition, the log drive era left sections of river overwidened and occasionally armored, the overwidened channels result in lowwer flow depths. For a given flow, lowwer flow depths combined with low bed slopes result in both lowwer boundary shear stress and unit stream power (impelling forces). The low boundary shear stress limits the size of sediment that can be mobizied while armored beds require higher boundary shear stresses to be mobilized. Low unit stream power limits the river's ability to maintain these sediments in motion once they are mobilized.

Where exceptions to the above discussion occur, the river is capable of forming geomorphic units, such as the pool and riffle crest near the upstream end of the S-Bend area of interst.
